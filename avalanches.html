Avalanches

What is an Avalanche?

An avalanche is a rapid flow of snow down a mountain or slope, usually triggered by natural forces (like heavy snowfall, temperature changes, or earthquakes) or human activity (like skiing or snowmobiling). Avalanches can be extremely dangerous, burying everything in their path under tons of snow.

How do they happen?

Avalanches occur when the snowpack becomes unstable and a layer of snow slides down due to gravity. This instability can be caused by: Heavy snowfall or rain adding weight to the snowpack Rapid temperature changes weakening the snow layers Wind piling snow onto slopes Vibrations from earthquakes, wildlife, or human activity

Types of Avalanches

Slab Avalanche – The most dangerous type, where a solid layer of snow breaks away and slides down as a block. Loose Snow Avalanche – Starts small and grows as it moves, creating a fan-like shape. Wet Snow Avalanche – Happens in warmer conditions when melting snow becomes heavy and slides. Cornice Fall – When an overhanging mass of snow breaks off, triggering an avalanche below.

How to stay safe

Always check avalanche forecasts before heading into the mountains. Avoid steep slopes (30–45°) and areas with recent snowfall. Carry avalanche safety gear (beacon, probe, shovel, airbag backpack). Travel one at a time in risky areas and stay aware of escape routes.
